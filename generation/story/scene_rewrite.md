## XXI. The Rubicon Before the Rubicon

A sentry waved Crispus through without checking his name — Sulla had left instructions — and he walked the ordered rows of tents toward the command pavilion, passing soldiers who sharpened blades and said nothing, their silence more eloquent than any speech he'd heard in the Senate.

Sulla was writing when Crispus entered. He didn't look up immediately, which was itself a communication — the controlled pause of a man who managed every interaction, including the intervals between them. When he did look up, his face was composed in the way a blade is composed: everything unnecessary removed.

"Sit," Sulla said. "Wine?"

"Thank you."

Sulla poured with his own hand, which Crispus had learned to read as a signal of intimacy — or its performance. When Sulla wanted distance, slaves poured.

"You've spoken with Catulus," Sulla said. It wasn't a question.

"This morning. He's — managing."

"He's terrified. Catulus has spent his life in a system that required nothing of him but procedural competence, and now the procedure has failed, and he doesn't know what comes next." Sulla drank. "I do."

Crispus felt the stillness settle over him — the restlessness gone, the fidgeting gone, the way it always went in this man's presence. He set down his cup so Sulla wouldn't see his hand tighten around it.

"Tell me."

Sulla leaned back. The lamplight caught the angles of his face and made him look like something carved.

"Sulpicius used the assembly to strip me of a command the Senate lawfully granted. You understand the mechanism?"

"Tribunician legislation through the concilium plebis. Legally defensible. Constitutionally —"

"Constitutionally catastrophic. Yes. If this stands, then every senatorial assignment — every command, every province, every appointment — is subject to override by any tribune who can organize a mob. The Senate becomes advisory. The assemblies become auctions. And the man with the loudest voice in the Forum controls the army." Sulla paused. "That man, at present, is Gaius Marius. Who is insane."

"He's not —"

"He is. You've met him. You sat across from him and watched him oscillate between brilliance and paranoia within the span of a single sentence. Don't soften it for my benefit, Crispus. I need people around me who see clearly."

The rebuke was gentle. That made it land harder. Crispus said nothing for a moment, then: "What are you going to do?"

"I'm going to enter Rome."

The words sat in the tent like a third person. Crispus heard them and understood them and still needed a moment before their full weight settled.

"With the legions."

"With the legions."

"That has never —"

"No. It hasn't." Sulla's voice was even, almost conversational, as though they were discussing the vintage of the wine. "And the reason it hasn't is that every general before me operated within a system where the rules, however imperfect, were honored by all parties. Sulpicius has demonstrated that the rules are no longer honored. Marius has demonstrated that ambition without constitutional restraint faces no constitutional penalty. I am simply — extending the lesson."

"Scaurus would say you're destroying the thing you claim to defend."

Sulla smiled. It was not a warm expression. "Scaurus is dead. And Scaurus spent fifty years defending a system that murdered Drusus, failed the Italians, and produced Marius. I admired the man. I will not repeat his error."

Crispus leaned forward, elbows on his knees. "Walk me through what happens after. You enter Rome, you remove Sulpicius — then what? You can't hold the city with legions indefinitely. You need the eastern command."

"Correct. I remove the men who broke the system. Sulpicius. Marius's key agents. Not indiscriminately — I have lists, and the lists are precise. I restore the Senate's authority to assign commands. I take the eastern army, as originally granted. I defeat Mithridates. And I return to finish the reconstruction."

"The lists," Crispus said. "You're talking about proscription."

"I'm talking about a mechanism. Targeted removal of political enemies combined with confiscation and redistribution of their property. The property funds the reconstruction. The removal ensures it isn't undone. It is a tool, Crispus. You are a man who understands tools."

"It's killing."

"Yes."

"Organized, systematic killing of Roman citizens."

"Of Roman citizens who have destroyed the constitutional order and will destroy it again if permitted to survive. Yes." Sulla refilled his cup. His hand was perfectly steady. "You're looking for me to flinch. I won't. I've thought about this longer than you've been involved in politics. Every alternative ends worse. Negotiation fails — we've watched it fail. Legislation fails — they killed Drusus. Electoral politics fails — Marius buys tribunes. What remains is force applied with precision, followed by reform, followed by withdrawal. I intend to do all three."

"And if the precedent —"

"The precedent is already set. Sulpicius set it when he used mob violence to override a lawful command. I am not creating a precedent. I am responding to one." He met Crispus's eyes. "You can repeat Scaurus's objection as many times as you like. It will remain eloquent and it will remain irrelevant. The question is not whether Rome has changed. Rome has changed. The question is who rebuilds it."

Silence. Outside the tent, someone was driving a stake into hard ground — a rhythmic, purposeful sound, the camp settling into the configuration it would hold until the order came to move.

"You're asking me to follow you into something that can't be undone," Crispus said.

"I'm not asking you anything yet. I'm telling you what I'm going to do. When I'm finished telling you, I will ask you one question, and the question will be simple, and you will answer it honestly, because you are not a good enough liar to deceive me and I think you know that."

Crispus almost laughed. The impulse arrived — the defensive humor, the deflection — and for once he let it pass. Sulla had earned a serious answer. Or demanded one. The distinction, with Sulla, was academic.

"Ask."

Sulla set down his cup. The lamplight caught his eyes and found nothing uncertain in them.

"Are you with me?"

The tent. The camp. The quiet Italian night. The soldiers sharpening blades outside. The sound of the stake being driven into earth. All of it compressed into the space between the question and the answer, and in that space Crispus felt every conversation he'd had in the past year — Aurelia's warnings, Scaurus's lament, Catulus's managed terror, Norbanus's blunt decency, Atticus's measured exit, Drusus's doomed conviction, Silo's reframing of everything he'd believed about his own inheritance, Marius's distorted hunger — all of it arriving at once, all of it relevant, none of it sufficient.

"Yes," Crispus said.

Sulla nodded once. Not with pleasure. Not with relief. With the acknowledgment of a man who had expected this answer and had already incorporated it into his plans.

"Good. Finish your wine. Tomorrow will be long."

Crispus finished his wine. It was good wine. He noticed this, and noticed himself noticing, and understood that he was cataloguing small details because the large detail — what he had just committed to, what it meant, what it would cost — was too large to hold in his mind all at once.

He walked back through the camp in the dark. The sentries nodded. The soldiers slept or didn't sleep. Somewhere to the north, Rome waited — the city that had never been entered by force by its own army, the city that was about to learn what Scaurus had tried to tell it and Sulla had understood before anyone else: that the system's only enforcement mechanism was the agreement to be enforced, and the agreement was over.

Crispus's hand was steady. He checked.
